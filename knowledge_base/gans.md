# Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs)

## Overview

A **Generative Adversarial Network (GAN)** consists of two neural networks trained
simultaneously in direct competition with each other: a **generator**, which tries to
produce fake data realistic enough to fool the other network, and a **discriminator**,
which tries to correctly distinguish real data from the generator's fakes.

This adversarial setup is a fundamentally different training paradigm from everything
covered so far in this curriculum — instead of one network minimizing a single loss
against a fixed target (as in `loss_functions.md`), two networks are trained against each
other in a **minimax game**, where one network's improvement makes the other's job harder.

---

## Chunk: generator-and-discriminator

**ID**: `gans#generator-and-discriminator`

**Generator (G):** Takes a random noise vector z (sampled from a simple distribution,
e.g., Gaussian) as input, and outputs a synthetic data sample:

```
x_fake = G(z)
```

The generator never sees real training data directly — it only ever receives feedback
indirectly, through whether the discriminator was fooled by its output.

**Discriminator (D):** Takes a data sample (either real, from the training set, or fake,
from the generator) and outputs a single probability estimating how likely that sample is
to be real:

```
D(x) → probability that x is real, in (0, 1)
```

This is a standard binary classification task, similar in structure to what's covered in
`loss_functions#cross-entropy` — except its training data is dynamically generated by
another network that keeps changing.

**The adversarial relationship:** The discriminator is trained to correctly classify real
vs. fake as accurately as possible. The generator is trained to produce fakes that make the
discriminator *fail* — i.e., that get classified as real. Each network's training signal
comes directly from the other network's current behavior.

---

## Chunk: the-minimax-objective

**ID**: `gans#the-minimax-objective`

The GAN objective is formalized as a minimax game:

```
min_G  max_D  V(D, G) = E[log D(x)] + E[log(1 - D(G(z)))]
```

- The **discriminator** tries to **maximize** this objective: it wants D(x) close to 1 for
  real data, and D(G(z)) close to 0 for fake data (so log(1 - D(G(z))) is large).
- The **generator** tries to **minimize** this same objective: it wants D(G(z)) close to 1
  — meaning it wants the discriminator to be fooled into thinking its fakes are real.

**Alternating training procedure:**

1. Fix the generator's weights. Update the discriminator's weights via gradient
   descent/ascent (see `optimizers.md`) to better distinguish real from fake.
2. Fix the discriminator's weights. Update the generator's weights via gradient descent to
   better fool the now-updated discriminator.
3. Repeat.

**Key insight — the loss depends on a moving target:** Unlike a standard supervised
network where the target label is fixed throughout training, the generator's "target"
(what counts as a good fake) is defined by the discriminator's current state, which is
itself constantly changing as it trains. This mutual dependency is what makes GAN training
notoriously less stable than the single-network training covered in `optimizers.md` and
`gradient_descent.md`.

## Chunk: training-instability

**ID**: `gans#training-instability`

GAN training is well known to be difficult to stabilize, for reasons specific to the
adversarial setup:

**Mode collapse:** The generator discovers that a small number of outputs (or even just
one) reliably fools the current discriminator, and starts producing only that limited
variety of output rather than the full diversity present in the real training data. Since
the generator only receives feedback through whether it fools D, it has no explicit
incentive to be diverse unless the training dynamics push it there.

**Vanishing gradients for the generator:** If the discriminator becomes too good too
quickly — confidently and correctly rejecting nearly all of the generator's fakes — the
term log(1 - D(G(z))) saturates (D(G(z)) stays close to 0), producing very small
gradients for the generator to learn from. This connects back to the saturation issue
discussed in `activation_functions#sigmoid`, where a discriminator's confident, near-0 or
near-1 outputs correspond to the flat, near-zero-derivative regions of its output
activation.

**Balance is delicate:** If the discriminator is too weak, it gives the generator poor,
uninformative feedback. If the discriminator is too strong, the generator's gradients
vanish. Successful GAN training requires the two networks to improve at roughly matched
paces — neither dominating the other for long stretches.

## Chunk: gans-vs-autoencoders

**ID**: `gans#gans-vs-autoencoders`

Both GANs and autoencoders (`autoencoders.md`) are generative-adjacent architectures, but
they differ fundamentally in training signal and purpose:

| Aspect                     | Autoencoder                                                                                   | GAN                                                |
| -------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------- |
| Training signal            | Direct reconstruction loss vs. known input                                                    | Indirect, adversarial signal from a second network |
| Number of networks trained | One (encoder + decoder, jointly)                                                              | Two (generator and discriminator, competitively)   |
| Primary strength           | Learning compact, meaningful representations (`autoencoders#the-latent-space`)              | Generating sharp, realistic novel samples          |
| Typical weakness           | Reconstructions can be blurry (optimizing pixel-wise error tends to average over uncertainty) | Training instability, mode collapse                |

**Key insight:** An autoencoder is explicitly told "make the output match this specific
target" at every step (a well-defined loss). A GAN's generator is never told exactly what
a "good" output looks like — it only knows whether the discriminator, in its current
state, was fooled. This more indirect signal is part of why GANs can produce sharper,
more realistic-looking samples than typical autoencoders, but at the cost of much less
stable training.

## Socratic Question Seeds

1. "The generator never sees a single real training example directly — only whether the
   discriminator was fooled by its output. How is this training signal fundamentally
   different from the reconstruction loss used to train an autoencoder?"
2. "If the discriminator becomes extremely good very early in training — correctly
   rejecting the generator's fakes with near-100% confidence — what happens to the
   gradient the generator receives, based on what you know about saturating outputs?"
3. "In mode collapse, the generator produces only a narrow slice of the diversity present
   in the real data, but still successfully fools the discriminator. Why doesn't the
   generator have an automatic incentive to produce diverse outputs, given that its only
   goal is to fool D?"
4. "The GAN objective has the discriminator trying to maximize V(D,G) while the generator
   tries to minimize the exact same expression. What would happen to training if, instead
   of alternating between updating D and G, you updated both of their weights using
   gradients computed at the very same instant, from the same forward pass?"
5. "An autoencoder's decoder is trained with a clear, fixed target every step: reconstruct
   this specific input. What would the generator's job be if you tried to give it an
   equally explicit, fixed target for every noise vector z it receives?"
